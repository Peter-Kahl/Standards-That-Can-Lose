# Standards That Can Lose

### An Epistemic-Fiduciary Constitution for the University

## Abstract

This paper proposes that the university be constituted as an epistemic fiduciary in respect of its evaluative powers — the powers by which it admits, assesses, credits, promotes, funds and recognises — and that the defining incident of that constitution be a single structural requirement: that each such standard remain capable of losing, defeasible by what happens to those it governs, through a route the standard’s holder does not control. It argues not that universities are fiduciaries of knowledge as a matter of positive law — they are not — but that they should be so constituted, and it works out what that constitution requires. The resulting model is named epistemocracy, in a sense deliberately opposed to the word’s established grain: not rule by those certified as knowing, but governance in which epistemic authority is institutionally answerable and no recognised knower holds finality. The model is grounded in fiduciary answerability, epistemic plurality, and distributed credibility. Building on fiduciary theory (Frankel, Smith, Miller), virtue epistemology (Fricker, Zagzebski, Medina), and critical pedagogy (Freire, Darder), I diagnose the epistemic crisis of the contemporary university through three critical terms — optocratic drift, fiducial hollowing, and epistemic inversion — which identify how institutions privilege optics over substance, hollow fiduciary duties into public relations, and collapse plurality into singular figureheads.

The fiduciary claim is advanced as a proposal for how the university ought to be constituted, not as a description of English law: fiduciary status follows an undertaking of loyalty (Bristol and West Building Society v Mothew), and no such undertaking presently runs from the university to those its evaluative standards govern. Against this backdrop I propose epistemocracy as a structural reform whose primary mechanism is the periodic review of recognition standards against later evidence — admitted through a three-tier evidential architecture, in which later success among the excluded triggers review, a class-level pattern shifts the burden, and defeat requires that pattern to exceed a pre-specified error rate — supported by oversight bodies that the reviewed authority does not appoint, resource, instruct or dissolve, and which any affected party has standing to compel. The proposal is set against the oversight architecture the sector actually builds, which reproduces the pathologies it is meant to answer: bodies no beneficiary has standing to invoke, appointed by the institution they review, defended by the plurality of their composition rather than by independence from what they examine, and justified by their capacity to produce legitimacy — the defect Michael Power identifies in audit generally.

The model rests instead on exposure: the requirement that an evaluative standard remain capable of losing, by a route its holder does not control. Two consequences follow, and both are uncomfortable. Transparency, disclosure and reason-giving, though necessary and valuable, cannot by themselves supply the missing check, because a standard’s holder cannot exhaustively and independently validate the standard under which their own representing proceeds. And a body performing the function proposed here will frequently reduce institutional legitimacy rather than restore it — which is the point, and the test of whether it is working. The paper specifies what follows when a standard loses, and provides that where the operative standard was constituted outside the institution applying it — by a research-assessment framework, accreditor or funder — the finding must travel to that constitutor rather than terminate at the point of application. Beyond academia, epistemocracy provides a transferable framework for corporations, NGOs and democratic institutions, on conditions stated in Chapter 8.
